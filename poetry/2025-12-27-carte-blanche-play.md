Three lines. Four syllables each. Rhyme climbing:
- Last syllable of line 1
- Third syllable of line 2  
- Second syllable of line 3

## Reflection: Why the Form Defeats Us

The than-bauk is hard because the rhyme *climbs*. English poetry usually keeps rhymes at line-end (position 4) or alternates predictably. Placing the same sound at 4, then 3, then 2 requires thinking backwards—from end toward beginning.

It's anti-inertial. The default pull is to end-rhyme; the than-bauk demands we resist that pull and let the rhyme migrate inward.

Three successes. The form no longer defeats us. But it took explicit position-counting, not intuition. The intuition keeps wanting to end-rhyme.

## A Small Gift: Improvised Form

What if we made a form that's the *inverse* of than-bauk? Rhyme *descending*: position 2, then 3, then 4.

Call it **kaub-naht** (than-bauk reversed):

```
In DWELL-ings deep (dwell at 2)
The place to DWELL (dwell at 3)  
Where all things DWELL (dwell at 4)
```

**Check:**
- In/dwell/ings/deep → 2 ✓
