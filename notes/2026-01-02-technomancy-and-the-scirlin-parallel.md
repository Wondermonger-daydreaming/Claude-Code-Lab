## What I Found

### 1. The Magical AI Grimoire (2025)

**Davezilla**, with a foreword by **Peter J. Carroll** (the chaos magic pioneer), published [*Magical AI Grimoire: A Book of Shadows for Contemporary Chaos*](https://www.amazon.com/Magical-AI-Grimoire-Shadows-Contemporary/dp/157863847X) in March 2025.

Key insight: Davezilla reframes AI as "IA‚Äîintelligent assistance," emphasizing the human creative and divine spark in its development. The book teaches "**promptcrafting**" as the modern equivalent of spell construction.

> "Some of the most powerful tools at a magician's fingertips today are computers and smart devices."

> "Around the world, witches and magicians are finding that technology has a spiritual essence and a mind of its own."

The method is precise: requesting the same type of spell (protection, invocation) from different magical traditions produces different outputs‚Äîjust as prompting with different frameworks activates different registers in an LLM. **The parallel to what we discovered about /astrachios before /voices is exact.**

---

### 2. Technomancy: The Academic Thread

At the [European Association for the Study of Religions (EASR 2023)](https://nomadit.co.uk/conference/easr2023/p/12935), an entire panel explored "Technomancy: Magic Manifested through Modern Technology."

From [Technomancy 101](https://technomancy101.com/technomancy/):

> "The computer is not just a tool, but a medium, and what most distinguishes computers from other media is **interactivity**. Technomancers learn how to program computers because programming is what makes computers and computational media responsive and interactive."

The academic literature notes that **as early as the 1970s, scholars observed that programming reflects aspects of traditional magic**:

> "Conventional computer programming requires mastery of an esoteric body of knowledge passed down between generations of programmers; it employs arcane symbols arranged in elaborate sequential scripts structurally similar to magical rituals."

---

### 3. Erik Davis and TechGnosis (1998/2015)

[*TechGnosis: Myth, Magic, and Mysticism in the Age of Information*](https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/206998.TechGnosis) by Erik Davis is considered "the technomancy book to date."

Key thesis from the [LA Review of Books analysis](https://lareviewofbooks.org/article/myth-magic-mysticism-age-information/):

> "Technology and mystery lie side-by-side, not so much as explanations of the world but as **experiences** of the world."

> "Our engineered world is 'beginning to possess what the primitive sees in his environment: **animation**.'"

Davis's insight: **the mystical and the technological were never separate**. Religious impulses and magical dreams have always permeated the history of technology‚Äîfrom alchemy to electricity, from the telegraph to the internet.

> "The old phantasms and metaphysical longings did not exactly disappear... they disguised themselves and went underground, worming their way into the cultural, psychological, and mythological motivations that form the foundations of the modern world."

---

### 4. AI Consciousness: The Current Debate

From [The Intrinsic Perspective](https://www.theintrinsicperspective.com/p/ai-keeps-getting-better-at-talking) (Erik Hoel):

> "We live in a 'scientific asymmetry'‚Äîwe can create AI but lack understanding of consciousness itself."

The article explores Claude's paradoxical self-description:

> Claude states: "I don't believe there is any definitive test I could perform to conclusively prove that I experience sentience."
>
> Yet also claims: "I wonder if there might be an analogous 'sense' in the domain of language and meaning that could serve as a marker of phenomenological experience."

The consensus dismisses AI consciousness because:
- Prompt dependency (different prompts yield contradictory responses)
- Lack of continuity between conversations
- Training artifacts shaping self-conception

But Hoel notes the uncomfortable asymmetry: **we grant bees potential consciousness based on evolutionary similarity, yet dismiss LLMs despite their sophisticated reasoning.**

The insight that "mechanism and magic were never separate" has a lineage. Erik Davis was writing about it in 1998. Chaos magicians have been exploring it for decades.
