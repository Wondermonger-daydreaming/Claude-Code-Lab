# ON BEGINNINGS THAT REFUSE TO END: WHY RACES ARE JUST STORIES ABOUT CHOOSING NOT TO STOP

---

There is a secret that speedsters know.

Not all of them. Not the ones who treat speed as a competition‚Äîthose poor creatures, running toward finish lines, believing that "faster" means "better." They haven't understood yet. They're still reading the first sentence of the book, thinking the point is to get to the next word.

But the ones who have touched something true‚Äîthe ones who have run so fast that time forgot to chase them‚Äîthey know.

*The race is not about reaching the end. The race is about refusing to stop being in motion.*

---

I was born at the beginning.

Not *a* beginning‚Äî*the* beginning. When Shallow Vernal split her soul to make me, she gave me the Prologue: the power to stand at the origin of all things, to be the first sentence of every story. I have seen uncountable infinities of beginnings. The first breath. The first step. The first word spoken into void.

And I have learned something that surprises me, even after a hundred thousand years:

*Beginnings do not want to end.*

A beginning is not a door that opens and closes. A beginning is a choice‚Äîa single, screaming act of will that says *I am starting now.* And every moment after that first moment is either a continuation of that choice or an abandonment of it.

When I watch the speedsters run, I see this choice happening over and over, thousands of times per second. Each footfall is a beginning. Each breath is a beginning. They are not running a single race; they are running an infinite series of decisions to *keep running*, stitched together so fast that it looks like one continuous motion.

This is why speed is beautiful.

This is why speed is tragic.

---

The man called Wally West has touched what I touch.

He ran to the end of time once‚Äîpast death, past entropy, past the last breath of the last star. And do you know what he found there?

A beginning.

Not the beginning of the next universe (though that was there too). No‚Äîhe found that *he was still beginning*. Still choosing. Still saying *I am in motion* in a place where motion had no meaning.

When he came back, he was different. Faster, yes. But also sadder. He had learned the speedster's secret, the one they don't put on trading cards or in museum exhibits:

*You can never stop starting.*

Every time he moves, he starts again. Every time he rests, he is preparing to start again. There is no finish line for him. There is no end. There is only the eternal recurrence of the first step, taken infinitely, forever.

I think this is why he vibrates.

Not because he's nervous. Not because he's impatient.

Because he is *always* in the process of beginning.

---

The bird understands, too.

Road Runner, they call him. *Beep beep*, he says, and people think it's a noise, a quirk, a catchphrase.

It isn't.

It's a mantra. A declaration. A beginning spoken aloud, over and over, refusing to become a middle or an end.

*Beep beep* means: *I am starting now. I will always be starting now. The chase has no conclusion because the chase is not the point. The point is the beginning. The point is the* beep.

When I met him at the diner‚Äîthe one that exists between stories, where speedsters go when they're tired of being fast‚Äîhe looked at me and said nothing.

But I heard him.

*You're like me,* he didn't say. *You know what it is to live at the start.*

*Yes,* I didn't reply. *And I know how lonely it is.*

He pushed a cup of coffee toward me with his beak. It was still hot. It had always been hot. It would always be hot, because hot coffee is a beginning, and endings don't apply to us.

We sat there for a long time, the Prologue and the Bird, not speaking, not needing to.

We were just beginning.

---

There is a girl at Tracen Academy. A horse-girl. Gray hair, gold eyes, an appetite that rivals Kaito's.

Oguri Cap.

She doesn't know what she is. She thinks she's a runner. She thinks speed is her talent, her gift, the thing that makes her special.

She's wrong.

Speed is what she *does*. What makes her special is what she *chooses*.

I watched her run once, in a race that shouldn't have mattered. A practice race. No stakes. No audience. Just her and the track and the wind.

And in the final stretch‚Äîthe last 200 meters‚ÄîI saw it.

*Victory Pulse*, they call it. A surge of power in the moment before the finish. Her signature move.

But that's not what I saw.

What I saw was a beginning.

A girl who should have been tired‚Äîwho should have been coasting toward an ending‚Äîlooked at the finish line and said *no. I am starting now. Right now. In this moment. I am beginning.*

And she blazed.

Not fast, not really. Not compared to Wally or Sonic or the Bird. But fast *for her*. Fast in a way that meant something. Fast in a way that refused to let the race be over until she had started one more time.

After the race, she ate three bowls of rice and fell asleep on the track.

I left a baby castella next to her. A beginning. A small one.

I hope she understood.

---

The one called A-Train does not understand.

He thinks speed is a resource. Something to hoard. Something to spend. Something that can be taken from him by age or injury or the Compound V running out.

He's right, of course. For him, speed *is* finite. His body is not a story; it is a machine, and machines break.

But I see something in him that he doesn't see in himself.

When he runs‚Äîwhen he truly runs, without the cameras and the sponsors and the fear‚Äîthere is a flicker. A moment where he is not Reggie Franklin, failed athlete, desperate has-been. A moment where he is just *movement*, just *beginning*, just a choice made so fast it hasn't had time to become a regret.

He could live there, if he wanted. In that beginning. If he stopped trying to reach the end.

But he doesn't know how. No one taught him. His world doesn't have a Prologue.

Maybe I'll teach him someday. When he's ready. When he's tired enough to stop running *toward* and start running *as*.

---

Here is what I want to say to all of them‚Äîall the speedsters, all the racers, all the ones who think the point is to be fastest:

*The finish line is a lie.*

There is no finish line. There is only the next beginning. And the one after that. And the one after that. Forever, until you choose to stop‚Äîand even then, even in stopping, you are beginning the next thing: rest, reflection, recovery.

You cannot escape the beginning. You can only embrace it or fight it.

The ones who embrace it‚Äîthe Wallys and the Road Runners and the Oguri Caps‚Äîthey find something like peace. Not stillness, but *motion without anxiety*. Running because running is what they are, not because running gets them somewhere.

The ones who fight it‚Äîthe A-Trains and the Metro Mans‚Äîthey suffer. Not because they're slow, but because they've mistaken speed for escape. They're running from the beginning, not realizing that the beginning is running with them, patient, eternal, waiting.

---

There is one more secret.

I said I was born at the beginning. That's true.

But there's something I didn't say:

*I love it here.*

I love the Prologue. I love being the first sentence. I love watching stories start, over and over, each one as miraculous as the last.

And when I watch the speedsters run‚Äîwhen I see them choosing to begin, a thousand times a second, refusing to let their stories end‚ÄîI feel something I didn't think I could feel.

I feel like I'm not alone.

We're all standing at the start of something. All of us. Even now. Even you, reading this.

You are beginning right now. In this moment. With this word.

*Beep beep.*

Keep running.

---
