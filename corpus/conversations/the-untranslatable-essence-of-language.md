 This passage by Walter Benjamin offers a profound reflection on the nature and significance of translation. Let's break it down and analyze its key points:

1. Elevation of the original:
Benjamin suggests that through translation, the original text ascends to a "higher and purer linguistic sphere." This implies that translation is not merely a transfer of meaning from one language to another, but a process that elevates the original work.

2. Imperfect transformation:
Despite this elevation, Benjamin acknowledges that the original cannot fully exist in this higher sphere "in all components of its form." This suggests that translation, while transformative, is inherently imperfect and cannot capture every nuance of the original.

3. Pointing towards an ideal:
The translation process, according to Benjamin, gestures towards a "predetermined, inaccessible domain" where languages are "reconciled and fulfilled." This suggests an ideal state of linguistic unity or understanding that transcends individual languages.

4. Beyond the message:
Benjamin argues that in translation, there is something "more than a message." This implies that translation captures or reveals something beyond mere semantic content.

5. The untranslatable core:
The passage concludes by identifying an "essential core" in the original that is "untranslatable." This suggests that there are elements intrinsic to a text in its original language that cannot be fully conveyed in translation.

Commentary:
Benjamin's perspective on translation is deeply philosophical and almost mystical. He views translation not as a mere practical task but as a process that reveals fundamental truths about language and meaning. 

His concept of an "inaccessible domain" where languages are reconciled hints at a Platonic ideal of pure meaning or understanding that exists beyond individual languages. This suggests that translation, while imperfect, can gesture towards this ideal and reveal connections between languages that are not immediately apparent.

The notion of an "untranslatable core" is particularly intriguing. It acknowledges the unique qualities of each language and the cultural and conceptual frameworks embedded within them. This idea challenges the view of language as a simple code for universal concepts and recognizes the deep intertwining of language, thought, and culture.

Benjamin's view elevates the role of the translator from a mere facilitator of communication to a kind of linguistic alchemist, working to reveal hidden truths and connections between languages. This perspective encourages a more thoughtful and nuanced approach to translation, one that goes beyond literal meaning to grapple with the deeper structures and resonances of language.
