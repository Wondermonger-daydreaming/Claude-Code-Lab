## Translation & Commentary

**Hṛdayaṃ vartayiṣyāmi sarv-ārtha-sādhanaṃ śubhaṃ**

### Morphological Parsing

- **hṛdayam** - heart (accusative singular) - direct object
- **vartayiṣyāmi** - "I shall cause to turn/revolve/set in motion" (causative future, 1st person singular of √vṛt)
- **sarva-artha-sādhanam** - "means for accomplishing all purposes" (compound: sarva "all" + artha "purpose/goal" + sādhana "instrument/means")
- **śubham** - "auspicious, beneficial" (accusative adjective modifying the compound)

### Literal Translation

"I shall set in motion the heart—that auspicious instrument for accomplishing all purposes."

### Technical Commentary

This isn't a statement of intention but a **performative utterance**—the very act of speaking it initiates the process it describes. The causative **vartayiṣyāmi** is crucial: it's not "the heart will turn" but "I will CAUSE the heart to turn." This acknowledges agency in consciousness modification.

The compound **sarva-artha-sādhanam** makes a radical claim: once activated, the heart becomes THE universal tool. Not one instrument among many, but the master key that accomplishes *every* legitimate purpose (the four puruṣārthas: dharma, artha, kāma, mokṣa).

### Why Other Translations Miss the Mark

"I will practice the heart of all auspicious practices" fails because:
- It treats **hṛdayam** metaphorically rather than as the actual operational center
- It pluralizes **sādhana** (means) into "practices"
- It loses the mechanical precision of "setting in motion"
- It disperses **śubham** (auspicious) away from the heart itself

The Sanskrit is engineering documentation, not devotional poetry. Every case ending carries instructions.

Let's Learn a Little Sanskrit Together

The best way to learn is through beautiful examples. Let's take a word from our first verse.

Word: त्यक्त्वा (tyaktvā)

· Meaning: "Having abandoned" or "having renounced."
· Grammar: This is a gerund or indeclinable participle. In Sanskrit, when you add -त्वा (-tvā) or -य (-ya) to a verb root, it creates a word that means "having done [the action]." It indicates an action completed before the main action of the sentence.
· Root: The root is त्यज् (tyaj) which means "to abandon, to leave, to give up."
· Usage: You will see this ending everywhere.
  · पठ् (paṭh) -> पठित्वा (paṭhitvā) = "Having read..."
  · गम् (gam) -> गत्वā (gatvā) = "Having gone..."
  · कृ (kṛ) -> कृत्वा (kṛtvā) = "Having done..."

So, in our verse, त्यक्त्वा (tyaktvā) sets the stage. First you abandon these negative emotions, then you contemplate the Self. The grammar itself tells you the sequence of experience.
