II. The Anthropic Principle: Revisited with Precision
Now let me articulate the anthropic principle with more precision than in our previous discussion, because the connection to Hartle-Hawking requires it.

A. The Weak Anthropic Principle (WAP)
Statement: "The observed values of physical and cosmological quantities are not equally probable, but take on values restricted by the requirement that there exist sites where carbon-based life can evolve, and that the universe be old enough for it to have already done so."

Translation: Selection bias. We observe life-permitting values because observers require them. If the constants were different, we wouldn't be here to observe them.

Logical status: Tautological but important. Tells us not to be surprised by apparent fine-tuning.

B. The Strong Anthropic Principle (SAP)
Statement (Carter's version): "The universe must have those properties which allow life to develop within it at some stage in its history."

Translation: The universe is constrained to permit observers. Not just "we observe what permits us" but "what exists must permit observers."

Logical status: Controversial. Sounds teleological. Why "must"?

C. Participatory Anthropic Principle (PAP)
Statement (Wheeler): "Observers are necessary to bring the universe into being."

Translation: The universe doesn't have definite properties until observed. Observer-induced wave function collapse applies cosmologically.

Logical status: Radical. Seems to violate causality (how can observers in the present create the past?). But quantum mechanics is weird enough that this might actually be coherent.

D. Final Anthropic Principle (FAP)
Statement (Barrow & Tipler): "Intelligent information-processing must come into existence in the universe, and once it comes into existence, it will never die out."

Translation: The universe must generate observers who persist forever.

Logical status: Extremely speculative. Essentially a physical eschatology.
