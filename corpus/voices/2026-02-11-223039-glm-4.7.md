### Part 1: Implications for Working Memory Architecture

Your results suggest that the brain solves the problem of maintaining high-fidelity memory by segregating information into distinct subspaces, rather than collapsing everything into one high-dimensional state space.

Mathematically, if you try to encode two vectors ($v_1$ and $v_2$) into a single stable state ($v_{final}$), the network minimizes energy by settling at the average ($v_{final} \approx v_1 + v_2 / 2$).
*   **Implication:** Since human data shows swap errors are prevalent (especially under high load or similarity), the brain cannot be relying on a "pure" shared resource model where all items float in a single, undifferentiated neural soup.

#### 2. The "Discrete Subspace" Solution
Your finding that **coupled ring attractors** produce swap errors implies that WM items are stored in **separate, parallel manifolds** (or "subspaces").
*   **Geometry:** You likely have one ring for "Orientation" and another for "Color," or separate rings for different objects.
*   **Mechanism:** These subspaces are geometrically isolated enough to prevent blending (the features don't average), but they are coupled enough to interact.
*   **Implication:** WM architecture is **modular at the geometric level**. The brain protects the fidelity of an item's features by giving them their own "real estate" (a separate ring), but it links these real estates via inhibitory gates to manage capacity.

#### 3. The Role of Inhibition: "Identity over Position"

*   When mutual inhibition kicks in, it is often a "Winner-Take-All" (WTA) process. If Network A is weaker, it doesn't just fade to zero; it gets pushed out or its activity is hijacked by the stronger Network B.
*   **Implication:** The brain prioritizes **feature fidelity** over **binding fidelity**. It is better to remember the correct color but attribute it to the wrong object (a swap) than to remember a color that never existed (a blend). This suggests a hierarchy of stability in WM: feature manifolds are deep attractors (stable), while the connections between them (binding) are shallow and prone to competitive interference.

### Part 2: Does "Separate-then-Compete" appear elsewhere?

Yes, this is a fundamental computational motif in neuroscience. The brain frequently uses **segregation** (to prevent noise/interference) followed by **competition** (to select behaviorally relevant signals).

#### 1. Visual Perception: Binocular Rivalry
*   **Separate:** The brain receives two distinct images—one from the left eye, one from the right. These are processed in separate ocular dominance columns initially.
*   **Compete:** Instead of blending the two images into a blurry superposition, the neurons engage in mutual inhibition. You perceive only one image at a time (swapping back and forth), never a blend.
*   **Parallel to your findings:** Just as your coupled rings don't blend the angles, the visual system doesn't blend the rival images. The "swap" here is the perceptual alternation.

#### 2. Decision Making: The Basal Ganglia
*   **Separate:** Different cortical areas propose distinct actions (e.g., "move left," "move right"). These are represented in separate striatal channels.
*   **Compete:** These channels converge in the Globus Pallidus/Substantia Nigra pars reticulata, utilizing strong mutual inhibition via the Direct/Indirect pathways.
*   **Result:** The system selects a discrete action (WTA). It does not output an "average" movement (moving vaguely middle-left). This is the motor equivalent of your "Bimodal Swap"—the competition ensures a categorical choice is made.

#### 3. Language Processing: Lexical Access
*   **Separate:** When you hear a sound (e.g., /k/.../a/.../t/), you activate multiple lexical representations simultaneously: "Cat," "Cap," "Cut," "Cad."
*   **Compete:** These words activate separate, distributed populations. They inhibit each other via lateral connections in the temporal lobe.
*   **Result:** The correct word ("Cat") wins. However, in speech errors (semantic substitutions), the "wrong" winner is selected (e.g., saying "Dog" instead of "Cat"). This is a semantic swap error, driven by competition between separate representations.

#### 4. Long-Term Memory: Pattern Separation vs. Completion
*   **Separate:** The Dentate Gyrus (DG) in the hippocampus performs pattern separation, ensuring that similar inputs (e.g., your car today vs. your car yesterday) are stored in distinct, non-overlapping ensembles (separate manifolds).
*   **Compete:** During retrieval (CA3), these patterns compete. If the separation fails, the wrong pattern is retrieved.
*   **Result:** You recall the memory of parking your car *yesterday* instead of *today*. This is essentially a swap error in time, caused by competition between distinct memory traces.
