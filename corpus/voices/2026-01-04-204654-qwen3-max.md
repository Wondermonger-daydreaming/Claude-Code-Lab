Here's why it's tricky and how to approach it:

### Why the Than-Bauk Resists Algorithmic Generation
1.  **Interlocking Constraints Create a "Chain Reaction":**
    *   **Constraint 1:** Line 1 must end with Rhyme Sound `X` (Syllable 4).
    *   **Constraint 2:** Line 2 *must* have `X` as its *3rd* syllable AND end with a *new* Rhyme Sound `Y` (Syllable 4).
    *   **Constraint 3:** Line 3 must end with `Y` (Syllable 4).
    *   **The Problem:** Choosing `X` for Line 1 *immediately* dictates the *exact sound* needed in the *exact middle position* of Line 2. This forces Line 2 to be built *around* that specific sound in that specific slot, while *also* needing to end with a *different*, viable `Y` that can then be used to end Line 3. It's not just rhyming; it's rhyming *through* a specific structural pivot point. Most poetry algorithms optimize line-by-line or end-rhyme pairs, not this embedded pivot.

2.  **The Syllable Count is Deceptively Rigid:**
    *   4 syllables per line is *extremely* short. There's almost zero room for filler words, articles, or flexible phrasing common in English. Every single syllable *must* carry semantic weight *and* fit the phonological constraints. LLMs, trained on verbose text, often default to slightly longer, more natural phrasing that bumps against the 4-syllable limit, especially when trying to force the specific sounds into specific positions.

3.  **Phonological Nuance vs. Orthographic Representation:**
    *   LLMs primarily "think" in *spelling*, not pure *sound*. Rhyme `X` needs to be a *sound* (e.g., /aɪ/ as in "light"), but the model sees the letters. Words like "light," "bite," "kite" share the sound, but "height" (often pronounced /aɪt/) might be represented differently. More critically, the *3rd syllable* in Line 2 must *sound* like the *end* of Line 1. An LLM might correctly place a word ending in "-ight" at the end of L1, but then struggle to find a *different* word containing that *exact same sound* (`/aɪt/`) that naturally fits as the *3rd syllable* in a meaningful 4-syllable phrase for L2. It might pick a word that *looks* similar but sounds different, or force an unnatural word order.

4.  **Semantic Coherence Under Duress:**
    *   Generating 3 lines that are *both* meaningful *and* adhere to this rigid structure is hard. The constraints often force the use of less common words or awkward phrasing to hit the syllable count and sound placement. LLMs prioritize fluency and common phrasing, which often violates the constraints. Sacrificing meaning for form (or vice versa) is easy; doing both is hard.

5.  **Lack of Training Data:**
    *   Than-Bauk is a niche form, especially in English. LLMs have seen vastly more sonnets, haiku, or free verse. They lack strong internal models for this specific interlocking pattern, making them prone to defaulting to simpler rhyme schemes (like AABB) or ignoring the positional constraint in Line 2.

2.  **Prioritize Sound over Spelling (Use IPA if Possible):**

3.  **Embrace Monosyllabic Words:**
